# Component Basics

---

It's "components" (元件), not "original parts" (原件). Things like circuit components and control components are all "components", similar to minimal structural units.

This lesson covers the functions and roles of various "movable" components. We won't go into very deep content here, as this is just an introductory tutorial.

When learning, we often compare electric current to water flow, and then compare Redstone energy to electric current. However, in Slime Tech tutorials, we cannot make any analogies; we can only memorize things ourselves.

## Signals

Blocks of Redstone can produce stable signals for use by pistons and other components.

Observers can produce a 2gt signal when they detect a change or when they themselves move. A signal of this length can cause a sticky piston to extend a block without retracting it.

Some signal sources may also use detector rails with minecarts, generally used for propelling TNT duplicators.

You can use observers paired with note blocks, fence gates, or trapdoors to transmit signals; most of the time they are also used as switches for the entire machine.

_"Let's start directly" - by directly breaking the fence gate, an update is produced on the observer, which can also start the entire machine_

## Pistons

Pistons are divided into regular pistons and sticky pistons. Regular pistons extend when they receive a signal and retract when the signal is cut off. During extension, they can push blocks in front of them, with a push limit of 12 blocks. Sticky pistons work the same way, except that when receiving a signal normally, when the signal is cut off and the piston retracts, it can pull blocks back. Both push and pull limits are 12 blocks.

Blocks that cannot be pushed by pistons include containers, both types of obsidian, bedrock, and extended pistons. There are also some blocks that will be destroyed when pushed, such as plants and some Redstone components. Notably, glazed terracotta can be pushed but cannot be pulled back.

When sticky pistons receive a signal of 2gt or less in length, they will extend blocks without retracting them, so this can be used to create bidirectional flying machines.

Pistons also have a special property called "Quasi Connectivity", abbreviated as `QC`. Due to pistons being written as doors in the 1.5 era (**Note: this has been verified as incorrect**), signals from 5 spaces around the block above the piston can also maintain the piston's extension.

> **Note: there is an error here, which has been verified**
>
> 1. Pistons themselves do not have the _"written as doors"_ explanation; this claim is a rumor.
> 2. The explanation of `QC` here is incorrect. For details, see GTMC Update Theory [1.3.2 Properties of NC Updates, QC Activation, BUD Devices](https://beta.techmc.wiki/articles/block-update/update-concepts)

Pistons need both signal and update to extend. When a signal is transmitted directly to the piston, it simultaneously updates the piston, and the piston extends. When a signal is transmitted above the piston and the piston has not received an update, it will retain its original state. After updating the piston, the piston changes to the corresponding state. The update referred to here is `NC update` (Neighbor Change). See the update theory tutorial for details.

In the image, the Block of Redstone can provide a signal to the piston but cannot update the piston. At this time, the Block of Redstone first updates the note block, and the note block updates the piston. Therefore, the piston can extend.

![img.png](img/1.2.2-qc伸出.png)

_Most blocks in Java Edition are not "event listeners" that constantly check whether their state needs to change, but rather need updates from others to trigger their own detection once. So when a block is placed/state changes/powered, it updates surrounding blocks. Only at this time will surrounding blocks check their own state._

_Therefore, in your thinking, you must also shift from the idea of "components actively detecting" to "components passively detecting"_

## Other Blocks

Note blocks, fence gates, and trapdoors can change when they receive a signal, and this change can be detected by observers, thereby transmitting the signal onward. At the same time, note blocks, fence gates, and wooden trapdoors can also accept player clicks and act as switches.

When rails are pushed, if the target position can also maintain the current state, they will not drop; otherwise, they will be destroyed. (Maintaining the current state here refers to the rail's `shape` property. For example, if an inclined rail is pushed and there is no block for the inclined rail to attach to after pushing, the rail will drop)

TNT will be ignited when it receives a signal and update. Redstone lamps will light up instantly when they receive a signal and can maintain 4gt of time after losing the signal before going out.

## Blocks

What kind of component is a block? Indeed, it's not really one, but water systems cannot exist without pipes, and circuits cannot exist without wires... Blocks in Slime Tech serve this purpose.

### a) Sticky Blocks

The traditional sticky block is the `Slime Block`, also called the `Slime Block`. This is also the origin of the name "Slime Tech" (绿萌).

Slime Blocks can stick to each other and move together when interacting with pistons. They can also stick to various components and auxiliary blocks. Since the piston push limit is 12, generally Slime Blocks are connected to no more than 12 blocks.

Slime Blocks cannot stick to blocks that pistons cannot push, blocks that are destroyed when pushed, and glazed terracotta. Notably, glazed terracotta can be pushed but cannot stick.

![img.png](img/1.2.4-陶瓦.png)

Honey Blocks are new blocks added in version 1.15, providing Slime Tech players with more options. Honey Blocks have the same sticking properties as Slime Blocks but do not stick to Slime Blocks.

Honey Block surfaces are incomplete and cannot be powered or have rails attached.

### b) Functional Blocks

In Slime Tech, besides components, some blocks also have certain functions. For example, cauldrons and composters are often used as "seats" for flying machines. In addition, there are fences and walls used for collision box clipping, and various sized blocks used for precise positioning. These blocks are numerous and have complex classifications (after all, you can't imagine what designers stuff into machines to achieve certain functions). We will analyze them specifically when we encounter them.

### c) Auxiliary Blocks

Auxiliary blocks are roughly divided into these categories:

1. Placeholder blocks: glass, etc.

These blocks do not serve any functional purpose; they are only used to push (hold) other structures. They are the most common blocks.

2. Powerable blocks: smooth stone, iron blocks, and other solid blocks

On the basis of placeholder blocks, these blocks need to be able to be powered and are used for signal transmission.

3. Special sticking blocks: glazed terracotta

The special nature of glazed terracotta (commonly abbreviated as "terracotta") has been explained above and will not be repeated here.
