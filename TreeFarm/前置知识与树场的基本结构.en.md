## Prerequisites

> This is a brief introduction to the basic concepts for starting tree farm research. For simplification purposes, this section will contain some "incorrect" content. For more detailed explanations, please refer to the **Update Theory** and **Timing Theory** sections of GTMC.

### Updates

In Minecraft, updates are divided into **NC updates** and **PP updates**.

The main sources of **NC updates** in tree farms are **TT (Tile Tick) components** (generally: Redstone Repeaters, Comparators, Observers), **Redstone Dust**, **powered or activated rails** *\*the following sections will use "rails" to refer to them, as tree farms rarely involve the other two types of rails\**, **Pistons** and the blocks they push/pull, and **Note Blocks**.

Generally, it can be assumed that when certain states of the above components change (such as whether they are powered, activated, or the block's state), they send **NC updates** to:

- Redstone Dust (energy level change), second-order neighbors
- Repeaters, Comparators, Observers: first the block their output points to, then the neighbors of the block their output points to, excluding itself
- Horizontally placed rails: first its own neighbors, then the neighbors of the block below; diagonally placed rails: first its own neighbors, then the neighbors of the block below, and finally the neighbors of the block above
- Pistons: the neighbors of itself and the original position of the blocks it pushes/pulls
- Note Blocks: neighbors

These emit **NC updates** and can be responded to by **BUD**.

Since the main structure of tree farms is generally composed of Pistons, it can generally be assumed that all **NC updates** in tree farms are accompanied by **PP updates**. Observers detect **PP updates**.

In tree farms, pure PP updates (without NC updates) are generally provided by:

- Trapdoors/Fence Gates opening/closing
- Dispensers/Droppers/Hoppers changing activation state
- Glass Panes/Fences/Iron Bars/Walls changing connection state

> In the wireless redstone section, we will cover three very important signal transmission methods: tree power (through leaves, transmitting NC and PP updates), scaffolding power (through scaffolding, transmitting NC and PP updates), and wall power (through walls, vertically transmitting PP updates). By utilizing their characteristics well, we can greatly simplify the wiring in tree farms. More detailed content will be covered in the wireless redstone special topic.

### Timing

Timing in Minecraft is divided into **inter-tick timing** and **intra-tick timing**.

In Minecraft, each second is divided into 20gt (_the concept is not explained here_). Some components have fixed **macroscopic** delays, such as TT components: Repeaters (2-8gt), Comparators and Observers (2gt).
It can be assumed that except for TT components, other components involved in tree farms **do not have** macroscopic delays.

Each gt is divided into multiple phases, each executing different operations. The phases most relevant to tree farms (in order of execution) are:

- **Tile Tick** (TT), where TT component operations are executed
- **Block Event** (BE), where Piston and Note Block operations are executed
- **Entity Update** (EU), where entity-related calculations (such as vehicles or mobs) are executed
- **Tile Entity** (TE), where Hopper and block-related operations pushed/pulled by Pistons are executed
- **Async Task** (AT/NU), where player-related calculations are executed

Except for the components mentioned above, **instant components** execute their operations immediately after receiving an update (regardless of which phase the update is in). Common instant components include **Redstone Dust and rails**, **Fence Gates or Trapdoors**, **Note Blocks**, **Droppers and Dispensers**.

The execution order of TT components is determined by **macroscopic timing**, **TT priority**, and **sub-order**. It can be assumed that the execution order of TT components is:
Any Repeater >= Comparator pointing to a Comparator > general Comparator or Observer

BE components (Pistons and Note Blocks) execute operations in BE in the order they **receive updates and confirm the need to change state**. This order is sometimes called **depth**. Note Blocks do not increase depth (i.e., delay the execution order of objects they update within BE).

When analyzing timing, first analyze **macroscopic timing**, then analyze **the phase where the operation is located**, and finally analyze **the order of execution within that phase**.

In tree farms, it can be assumed that each Piston action (push/pull/0t) takes **3gt**, and Piston actions caused by **1gt and 2gt** powering are **4gt and 5gt** respectively.

## Basic Structure of Tree Farms

<!-- >Remember to add images to this section -->

In Minecraft 1.15 and above, the basic structure of tree farms includes:

- Bonemealing
- Trunk processing (sometimes divided into **main trunk processing** and **root processing**)
- Leaf processing
- Sapling recycling
- Block-to-drop conversion

> Most tree farms also have a **detection** module, used to detect sapling growth and trigger the tree farm, but this is not necessary for tree farms. We will encounter it in the next chapter.
>
> In Minecraft 1.15 and below, due to the special growth detection methods of jungle wood and acacia, sometimes a **height increase** module is added to tree farms; because spruce growth detection requires no logs within a 5x5 area, a **retractable wall** needs to be added. This article mainly discusses tree farms in 1.15 and above, so we will temporarily ignore these structures.

### Bonemealing

Generally, this involves **Dispensers** dispensing Bone Meal onto saplings.

Since Dispensers can store very little Bone Meal, generally Bone Meal is fed into Dispensers through **Hoppers or Droppers**, and an **unloader** is used to transfer Bone Meal from Shulker Boxes to the Hopper or Dropper chain.
