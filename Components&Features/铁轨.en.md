## Rail Updates

Except for plain rails, special rails (Powered Rails, Activator Rails, Detector Rails) all have special update ranges.

### Powered Rail/Activator Rail Updates

Powered Rails and Activator Rails have identical update behavior.[^1]

[^1]: Powered Rails and Activator Rails are both implemented by the `PoweredRailBlock` class, so their behavior is completely identical at the source code level. The different effects they produce on minecarts are determined and handled by the minecarts themselves, not by the rails.

When a Powered Rail/Activator Rail's activation state changes, it will emit NC updates in sequence with these positions as update sources:

1. One block above (if the rail is sloped)
2. Itself
3. One block below
4. Itself
5. One block below
6. One block above (if the rail is sloped)

In other words, for a flat Powered Rail/Activator Rail, it emits 4 NC updates with **itself->below->itself->below** as update sources;

For a sloped Powered Rail/Activator Rail, it emits 6 NC updates with **above->itself->below->itself->below->above** as update sources.

## Rail Activation Condition Detection

**All "rails" in this section refer to Powered Rails or Activator Rails**

### Activation Methods

There are two conditions that can activate a rail:

- Directly adjacent to a redstone signal source, called **direct activation**
- Among the 8 rails **connected** to itself (excluding itself), 1 rail is directly adjacent to a redstone signal source, called **indirect activation**

For example, if a rail is directly adjacent to a Block of Redstone, it is **directly activated**;

If a rail is connected to several other rails, forming a rail chain, and there is an adjacent Block of Redstone on the rail chain within 8 blocks of that rail, then that rail is **indirectly activated**.

### "Connected" Judgment Conditions

A rail that is not directly activated will use a "search" mechanism to determine whether it is indirectly activated. Specifically, it will search for whether there is a **connected**, **directly activated** rail within 8 blocks of itself.

To put it simply, imagine you're going to a restaurant. You would open a map, search for the restaurant, and see if you can walk there; the restaurant won't fly to you and tell you "I'm 800m away from you, you can walk over."

Rails work the same way. Each rail will "search" for qualifying signal sources on its own—that is, the rail will try to "walk" along the rail chain for up to 8 blocks to see if it can find a signal source—that is, whether it can find a rail directly connected to a signal source.

A very intuitive idea is: if the rail "walks" halfway and the rail chain is "broken," then it definitely can't continue walking.

When a rail searches for connected rails at a certain position, it will search for rails at **specific positions** with the **same direction** as itself based on its own **shape** (i.e., whether it is sloped). That is, whether a rail judges the next rail as connected to itself only depends on these three factors:

- Its own shape (whether it is sloped)
- The position of the next rail
- The direction of the next rail

And is independent of **the shape of the next rail**.

Flat rails will check these positions:

- Positions at both ends of the rail, at the same height as itself
- Positions at both ends of the rail, one block lower than itself

The range is shown by the red glass positions in the image:

![平铁轨连通](img/01-平铁轨的相连范围.png)

For example, when the flat rail on the far right in the image is "searching" to the left for directly activated rails, it will judge that the rail on the left is connected to itself. The first three cases in the image are "intuitive," while the last one is caused by **rails not considering the shape of the next rail when checking connectivity**:

![平铁轨连通例](img/01-平铁轨的相连例.png)

Sloped rails will check these positions:

- Position at the lower end of the sloped rail, at the same height as itself
- Position at the lower end of the sloped rail, one block lower than itself
- Position at the higher end of the sloped rail, one block higher than itself

The range is shown by the red glass positions in the image:

![斜铁轨连通](img/01-斜铁轨的相连范围.png)

For example, when the sloped rail on the far right in the image is "searching" to the left for directly activated rails, it will judge that the rail on the left is connected to itself. The first three cases in the image are "intuitive," while the last one is caused by **rails not considering the shape of the next rail when checking connectivity**.

![斜铁轨连通例](img/01-斜铁轨的相连例.png)

Combining the above two points, we can discover: rail "connectivity" is unidirectional: when rail A judges that rail B is connected to itself, rail B does not necessarily judge that rail A is connected to itself:

![单向性](img/01-相连的单向性.png)

From an intuitive perspective, in the rail chain shown in the image, the redstone signal can propagate from right to left, but cannot propagate from left to right; from the "search" perspective, this means: the sloped rail on the left judges that the flat rail on the right is not connected to itself, while the flat rail on the right judges that the sloped rail on the left is connected to itself.

Analyzing the positions where these two rails check for connectivity: in the image above, the horizontal positions corresponding to the orange glass are the positions where the sloped rail checks for connectivity, and the horizontal positions corresponding to the red glass are the positions where the flat rail checks for connectivity. That is, the sloped rail is at the checking position of the flat rail; while the flat rail is not at the checking position of the sloped rail, which causes **rail connectivity to be unidirectional**.

This can be used to create rail chains that can only transmit rail signals in one direction.

### Direction of "Search"

Rails follow a fixed order when searching for connected rails: independent searches are performed for both ends, and each search is unidirectional (no backtracking).

- **Search process:** First, continuously search outward from one end along that direction; during the search, the direction does not change (i.e., only "forward," no "backward"); after the search at that end is complete, repeat the same search process for the other end.
- **Direction rules:** North-south oriented rails always search south first, then north; east-west oriented rails search west first, then east.

Therefore, whether multiple rails can be determined as "connected" depends not only on distance, but also on the order of the search direction. This explains the difference shown in the diagram: in the first case, the rails on both sides of the Block of Redstone can be found and activated; in the second case, only the rail on the left side of the Block of Redstone can be activated, because the search direction on the right side cannot pass through a certain rail in the middle to reach the target position, and thus cannot find the directly activated rail.

![单向性](img/01-寻找的单向性.png)

As shown in the image, in the first case, the rails on both the left and right sides of the Block of Redstone can be activated.

In the second case, only the rail on the left side of the Block of Redstone can be activated. When the rail on the left side of the Block of Redstone searches **to the right** for directly activated rails, it will first find rail #2, then search **to the right** (down) to find rail #3.

When the rail on the right side of the Block of Redstone searches **to the left** for directly activated rails, it will also first find rail #2; but since the search direction at this time is **to the left**, and rail #3 is on the **right side** of rail #2, it cannot search for rail #3, and thus cannot find the directly activated rail, and cannot be activated.
